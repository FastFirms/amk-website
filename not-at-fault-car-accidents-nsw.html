What Is The Nominal Defendant In NSW?

The Nominal Defendant is a statutory body under the Motor Accidents Injuries Act 2017 that compensates people injured by unidentified vehicles (hit-and-run) or vehicles without CTP insurance in NSW. If the at-fault driver cannot be identified or traced, or was driving an unregistered/uninsured vehicle, your claim goes to the Nominal Defendant.

The most critical rule: for claims involving an unidentified vehicle, you must give notice to the Nominal Defendant within 28 days of the accident. This is one of the strictest deadlines in NSW personal injury law. If you were in a hit-and-run in NSW, do not wait — get advice within days of the accident.

02

What Happens If You Miss The 28-Day Nominal Defendant Notice?

Missing the 28-day notice for an unidentified vehicle does not automatically bar your claim, but you will need to satisfy the court that you had a reasonable excuse for the delay and that the Nominal Defendant is not prejudiced by the late notice.

Courts have accepted reasonable excuses including hospitalisation, serious injury preventing action, and genuine ignorance of the requirement. However, excuses are assessed strictly — "I didn't know about the deadline" without more is unlikely to succeed. The safest approach is to get advice immediately after any hit-and-run accident so notice is given on time.

03

How Do You Prove Your Claim Against The Nominal Defendant?

For unidentified vehicle claims, you must establish: (1) you made reasonable inquiries at the scene to identify the vehicle; (2) the unidentified vehicle caused the accident; and (3) you suffered compensable injury. A police report and witness evidence significantly strengthen the claim.

The Nominal Defendant will scrutinise whether you genuinely attempted to identify the vehicle at the scene. Any witnesses who saw the vehicle, its colour, make, or partial registration should be documented immediately. We advise clients on exactly what steps to take to protect a Nominal Defendant claim from the moment of first contact.

04

What Compensation Can A Not-At-Fault NSW Driver Claim?

As a not-at-fault claimant under MAIA 2017, you have access to the full range of damages: statutory benefits (medical, income support), plus common law damages for economic loss and non-economic loss once 10% WPI is established. There is no reduction for contributory negligence if you were not at fault.

Passengers in a vehicle are among the easiest cases — passengers are almost never at fault for a collision, so the full compensation scheme applies. Browse our NSW CTP recent cases to see real court outcomes for not-at-fault claimants.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions.

The driver who hit me drove off — what do I do now?

You have 28 days to give notice to the Nominal Defendant for hit-and-run claims in NSW. Report the accident to police immediately. Note any details you observed — colour, make, partial rego. Get legal advice the same day if possible.

The at-fault driver had no insurance — am I covered?

Yes. Uninsured vehicle claims go to the Nominal Defendant. Notice must be given within 3 months of the accident for uninsured (but identified) vehicles. Get advice promptly.

Can I claim if I was a passenger in the at-fault driver's car?

Yes. Passengers are almost never at fault and can claim against the CTP insurer of the vehicle they were travelling in, regardless of who drove. Your relationship with the driver — friend, family, employer — does not affect your right to claim. The insurer pays, not the driver personally.

What if both drivers were partly at fault?

NSW applies contributory negligence — your damages are reduced by your assessed percentage of fault but not eliminated. Courts assess the specific facts. Get advice before accepting any insurer's allocation of blame.
