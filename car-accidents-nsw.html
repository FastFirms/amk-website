How Does CTP Insurance Work In NSW?

Every registered vehicle in NSW carries Compulsory Third Party (CTP) insurance under the Motor Accidents Injuries Act 2017. When a driver causes an accident, injured people — drivers, passengers, pedestrians, cyclists and motorcyclists — make claims against the at-fault vehicle's CTP insurer.

CTP insurers in NSW are approved private insurers (NRMA, GIO, Allianz, QBE, Guild) — not a government body. SIRA (the State Insurance Regulatory Authority) oversees the scheme and sets the rules. The insurer must fund reasonable treatment from the moment a claim is lodged, and must make a liability decision within 13 weeks.

Unlike the old NSW scheme (MAA 1999), the 2017 Act introduced a two-stream model: fault-neutral statutory benefits for everyone, and fault-based common law damages for seriously injured claimants. Understanding which stream applies to your injuries — and how they interact — is where expert legal advice pays for itself.

02

What Statutory Benefits Can You Claim After A Car Accident In NSW?

All injured people — even those who were at fault — can access statutory benefits covering medical treatment, rehabilitation and weekly income support. Benefits are available immediately on lodging an Accident Notification Form (ANF), regardless of who caused the accident.

Weekly income support covers 95% of your pre-accident weekly earnings for the first 13 weeks, reducing to 80% from week 14 to 26. After 26 weeks, income support continues for claimants who were not at fault and still cannot work. Medical, hospital and rehabilitation expenses are covered at the rates set by SIRA. There is no dollar cap on treatment funding while liability is accepted.

03

When Can You Claim Common Law Damages In NSW?

Common law damages — compensation for pain and suffering (non-economic loss), lost income and future care — are available to claimants who were not at fault and whose injuries result in a Whole Person Impairment (WPI) of 10% or more. The 10% threshold applies to non-economic loss; economic loss damages do not have a WPI threshold.

WPI is assessed by a SIRA-accredited medical assessor using the AMA guides. For injuries like soft-tissue neck and back injuries that fully resolve, WPI is often below 10% — which is precisely why insurers push early settlements before your injuries are properly assessed. For more serious injuries (fractures, nerve damage, psychological trauma), 10% is regularly met. Don't accept an insurer's characterisation of your injuries as minor without independent advice.

04

What Are The Time Limits For A CTP Claim In NSW?

Lodge an Accident Notification Form (ANF) within 28 days to access early treatment funding. Lodge a formal Notice of Claim with the insurer within 3 months of the accident, or within 28 days of first consulting a lawyer. Court proceedings for common law claims must be commenced within 3 years of the date of the accident.

For Nominal Defendant claims (unidentified or uninsured vehicles), notice must be given within 28 days of the accident or within 3 months if there is a reasonable excuse. The 28-day rule for unidentified vehicles is strictly enforced — delay can permanently bar your claim. If you were in a hit-and-run, get advice within days, not weeks.

05

What Compensation Can You Claim?

For claimants who clear the 10% WPI threshold, common law damages cover: past and future lost income (including superannuation contributions), the cost of past and future care and assistance, medical expenses not already met by statutory benefits, and non-economic loss (pain, suffering, loss of amenity of life).

Non-economic loss is assessed on a scale: maximum is $782,500 (indexed annually), awarded only for the most catastrophic injuries. Economic loss is uncapped and based on your actual earnings and career trajectory. For young claimants with serious injuries, future economic loss is typically the largest component — which is why early settlement offers from insurers consistently undervalue claims.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions.

Can I claim if I was at fault in the accident?

Under the Motor Accidents Injuries Act 2017, statutory benefits (medical treatment and income support for up to 52 weeks) are available even if you were at fault. However, at-fault claimants cannot claim non-economic loss or future economic loss through common law damages. If you were not at fault, or only partly at fault, you have access to the full compensation scheme.

Do I need to lodge an Accident Notification Form?

You should lodge an Accident Notification Form (ANF) with the at-fault driver's CTP insurer within 28 days of the accident. This unlocks early treatment funding immediately. Missing the 28-day window does not bar your claim, but you will lose access to that early treatment pathway.

How long do I have to lodge a CTP claim in NSW?

You must lodge a Notice of Claim with the CTP insurer within 3 months of the accident (or 28 days after first consulting a lawyer). Missing this deadline doesn't automatically bar your claim if there is a reasonable excuse — but delays make everything harder. Get advice early.

Do I need 10% WPI to make a CTP claim in NSW?

No — the 10% Whole Person Impairment threshold applies only to common law non-economic loss damages (pain and suffering). Statutory benefits are available to all injured claimants regardless of impairment. Economic loss damages also generally have no WPI threshold.

Will I have to go to court?

Almost certainly not. Most NSW CTP claims resolve through negotiation, mediation or a Claims Assessment at the Personal Injury Commission. Court proceedings are a last resort — but having lawyers genuinely prepared to run a trial is what produces fair offers at the negotiating table.

What if the other driver fled the scene?

You can claim against the Nominal Defendant — a statutory body that compensates people injured by unidentified or uninsured vehicles in NSW. The notice deadline for unidentified vehicles is 28 days (strict). Don't delay.

What CTP insurers operate in NSW?

CTP insurance in NSW is provided by approved private insurers including NRMA Insurance, GIO, Allianz, QBE and Guild Insurance. Your claim goes to the insurer of the at-fault vehicle, not a government body. Each insurer has its own claims team — and all are focused on minimising payouts.
