How Do Cyclists Claim Compensation After A NSW Road Accident?

If you were hit by a motor vehicle, your claim runs under the Motor Accidents Injuries Act 2017 against the at-fault vehicle's CTP insurer. As a cyclist you are a "non-motorist" — you have access to statutory benefits (medical, income support) regardless of fault, and to common law damages if your WPI reaches 10%.

Lodge an Accident Notification Form with the at-fault driver's CTP insurer within 28 days to access early treatment funding. Lodge your formal Notice of Claim within 3 months. Exchange details with the driver at the scene, photograph the vehicle and road conditions, and get witness names. See a doctor even if injuries feel minor — cycling impacts often cause delayed-onset spinal and soft tissue injuries.

02

Can You Claim Against The Road Authority For A Cycling Injury In NSW?

Yes — if a defective road surface, missing signage, inadequate lighting or poorly designed infrastructure caused your accident, Transport for NSW or the relevant local council may be liable under the Civil Liability Act 2002. These are public liability claims, separate from CTP.

Road authority claims require evidence of the hazard's existence, the authority's knowledge (or deemed knowledge) of it, and their failure to remedy it within a reasonable time. Photographs of the hazard immediately after the accident are critical. Section 45 of the Civil Liability Act provides limited immunity to roads authorities, but it is not absolute — if the authority was aware of the particular risk and failed to act, liability can be established.

03

What Compensation Is Available For An Injured NSW Cyclist?

Under MAIA 2017 (vehicle collision): statutory benefits for medical expenses and income support, plus common law damages (past and future economic loss, care, non-economic loss) once 10% WPI is established. Under Civil Liability Act (road defect): full damages including economic loss and non-economic loss subject to the 15% threshold.

Cycling injuries — collarbone fractures, wrist fractures, traumatic brain injury, spinal injuries — regularly meet the relevant thresholds. Even where thresholds aren't met, statutory benefits can cover treatment costs and ongoing income support throughout your recovery.

04

What If The Driver Who Hit You Is Unidentified Or Uninsured?

Claims against unidentified or uninsured vehicles in NSW go to the Nominal Defendant. For unidentified vehicles, you must give notice within 28 days of the accident (this is very strict). For uninsured vehicles, you must give notice within 3 months. Get advice immediately if you don't know the driver's details.

You must also establish that you made reasonable inquiries to identify the vehicle at the time. A police report and any witness statements confirming you looked for the driver significantly strengthen a Nominal Defendant claim.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions.

Can a cyclist claim CTP in NSW if hit by a car?

Yes. Cyclists are protected persons under the Motor Accidents Injuries Act 2017 and can claim against the at-fault vehicle's CTP insurer for statutory benefits (regardless of fault) and common law damages (if not at fault and WPI ≥ 10%).

What if I was partly at fault for the bike accident?

Contributory negligence reduces your damages proportionally. As a cyclist, common contributing factors include not wearing a helmet or riding with traffic — courts assess the degree of fault on the specific facts. Get advice before accepting any insurer's assessment of fault.

Can I claim against the council for a pothole injury?

Potentially yes, under the Civil Liability Act 2002, though roads authorities have qualified immunity under s45. The key is showing the authority had actual or constructive knowledge of the hazard and failed to act. Evidence of prior complaints or reports about the same hazard is valuable.
