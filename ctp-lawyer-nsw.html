What Law Governs CTP Claims In NSW?

The Motor Accidents Injuries Act 2017 (MAIA 2017) replaced the old Motor Accidents Compensation Act 1999 scheme. SIRA (the State Insurance Regulatory Authority) administers the scheme and regulates approved CTP insurers: NRMA Insurance, GIO, Allianz, QBE and Guild Insurance.

The 2017 scheme introduced a two-stream model. Stream 1: fault-neutral statutory benefits available to all injured persons (including at-fault drivers) covering medical treatment, rehabilitation and weekly income support. Stream 2: fault-based common law damages for not-at-fault claimants whose injuries result in 10% or greater Whole Person Impairment. Understanding which stream applies — and the interaction between them — is fundamental to running a successful claim.

02

How Do You Lodge A CTP Claim In NSW?

Step 1: Lodge an Accident Notification Form (ANF) with the at-fault vehicle's CTP insurer within 28 days to access immediate treatment funding. Step 2: Lodge a formal Notice of Claim within 3 months of the accident (or 28 days of first seeing a lawyer). Step 3: The insurer investigates and makes a liability decision within 13 weeks.

To identify the at-fault vehicle's CTP insurer, you can check the vehicle's registration at Service NSW. Never provide a recorded statement to the at-fault insurer without legal advice — statements are used throughout the claim and can be taken out of context to minimise your compensation. Our team was trained by insurers; we know exactly what they look for.

03

What Is The 10% WPI Threshold In NSW CTP?

To access common law non-economic loss damages (pain and suffering) under MAIA 2017, a not-at-fault claimant must have a Whole Person Impairment of at least 10%, assessed by a SIRA-accredited medical assessor using the AMA Guides. Economic loss claims do not require meeting a WPI threshold.

WPI is assessed once injuries have stabilised. Insurers often push early settlements — before injuries are fully assessed — precisely because early settlement avoids the 10% threshold being met. Never accept a settlement offer before your treating specialists have given a clear prognosis. A claim settled prematurely for statutory benefits may be worth many times more in common law damages once properly assessed.

04

What Happens If The Insurer Disputes Liability Or Reduces Your Benefits?

Disputes about statutory benefits — liability decisions, treatment approvals, weekly payment amounts — go to the Personal Injury Commission (PIC) for merit review or dispute resolution. Common law disputes go to the District Court or Supreme Court depending on the amount claimed.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions.

Which insurers provide CTP in NSW?

Approved CTP insurers in NSW include NRMA Insurance, GIO, Allianz, QBE and Guild Insurance. Your claim is made to the insurer of the at-fault vehicle — not to SIRA or any government body. Each insurer has its own claims team and approach.

What is the difference between the old and new NSW CTP scheme?

The Motor Accidents Injuries Act 2017 replaced the Motor Accidents Compensation Act 1999 from December 2017. Key changes: fault-neutral statutory benefits for all (including at-fault drivers), a 10% WPI threshold for non-economic loss, and SIRA-regulated approved insurers. If your accident was before 1 December 2017, the old scheme applies.

Do I need a lawyer for a CTP claim in NSW?

For statutory benefits disputes, insurers fund your legal costs on success — so legal representation for those disputes costs you nothing from your compensation. For common law claims, having a lawyer dramatically affects the outcome. The insurer has experienced claims managers; you should too.

How long does a NSW CTP claim take?

Statutory benefits begin within weeks of lodgement. Common law claims typically resolve in 18 to 30 months once injuries have stabilised. We give you a realistic timeline at your free consultation.
