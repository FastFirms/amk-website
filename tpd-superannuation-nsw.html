What Is TPD Insurance And Do You Have It?

Total and Permanent Disability (TPD) insurance pays a lump sum if you become totally and permanently disabled and are unlikely to ever return to work. It is commonly held inside superannuation funds — whether industry funds like AustralianSuper, REST or HESTA, or retail funds through the major banks and insurers.

Most Australian workers have been enrolled automatically in a super fund with default TPD cover since their first job. Many people have multiple funds from different employers — and each fund may carry separate TPD cover. We check all of them. If you have stopped working due to injury or illness, there is a real chance you have an unclaimed lump sum waiting.

02

How Does TPD Work In NSW?

TPD insurance is governed by federal law — the Superannuation Industry (Supervision) Act 1993 (SIS Act) and each fund's trust deed and product disclosure statement (PDS). The definition of "total and permanent disability" varies between funds, which is why the exact wording of your policy matters enormously.

NSW residents use the same federal framework as the rest of Australia. Common definitions include "unable to ever engage in any occupation for which you are reasonably suited by education, training or experience" (any occupation) or "unable to return to your own occupation" (own occupation). Own occupation definitions are generally easier to satisfy. We obtain and analyse your policy documentation as part of your free cover check.

03

What If Your TPD Claim Was Declined?

Declined TPD claims can be appealed. Super fund trustees and their insurers regularly decline claims on the basis that the medical evidence doesn't meet the definition — but with the right specialist evidence and legal argument, many declined claims are overturned.

You can lodge an internal dispute resolution (IDR) complaint with the fund, escalate to the Australian Financial Complaints Authority (AFCA), or in appropriate cases commence court proceedings. AFCA is free to use and regularly rules in favour of claimants who were initially declined. We handle the entire process on a No Win, No Fee basis.

04

Can You Claim TPD And Workers Compensation In NSW?

Yes — TPD insurance and NSW workers compensation are separate entitlements. Receiving icare workers compensation benefits does not prevent you from also claiming TPD from your super fund. If your injury stops you working permanently, both claims should be pursued.

Similarly, a TPD payout does not automatically affect your icare entitlements or any Work Injury Damages claim. The interaction between schemes is nuanced — getting advice on the sequencing and structure of multiple simultaneous claims is where legal guidance produces the most value.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions.

How do I know if I have TPD cover?

Check your super fund's annual statement or log in to your fund's online portal. If you have worked for multiple employers you may have multiple funds — each potentially with separate TPD cover. We check all of them as part of your free cover review.

My TPD claim was declined — is that the end?

No. You can lodge an IDR complaint with the fund, escalate to AFCA, or commence proceedings. Many declined claims are overturned with better specialist medical evidence and legal submissions. We handle this on a No Win, No Fee basis.

Can I claim TPD and icare workers compensation at the same time?

Yes. They are separate entitlements. Receiving icare benefits does not prevent a TPD claim, and a TPD payout generally does not affect your icare entitlements. Both should be pursued if your injury stops you working permanently.

Is there a time limit for a TPD claim?

Super funds do not usually publish hard limitation periods for TPD claims, but delays can complicate evidence gathering and some policies have activity-of-daily-living requirements. Acting early also protects your premium status. Don't wait unnecessarily.
