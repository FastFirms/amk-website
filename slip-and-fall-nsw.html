Who Is Liable For A Slip And Fall In NSW?

Under the Civil Liability Act 2002 (NSW), the occupier of premises owes a duty of care to visitors to take reasonable care to prevent foreseeable risks of harm. If they knew or should have known about the hazard and failed to act, they can be liable for your injuries.

Supermarkets, shopping centres, councils, restaurants, hotels and private homeowners can all be occupiers. In Woolworths Ltd v Strong [2010] NSWCA, the court confirmed that supermarkets must have a systematic cleaning and inspection regime — not just clean when someone reports a spill. The quality of the defendant's inspection records is often central to the outcome.

02

What Evidence Do You Need For A Slip And Fall Claim?

CCTV footage, incident reports, witness statements, cleaning and inspection logs, photographs of the hazard, and your medical records are the foundation of every slip and fall claim. CCTV is overwritten within 24–72 hours at most venues — preserving it is the most urgent step after getting medical attention.

We write preservation notices to the defendant immediately upon being instructed. Report the incident to the occupier on the day and get a copy of the incident report number. Photograph the hazard if it is safe to do so. See a doctor even for injuries that feel minor at the time — spinal and soft tissue injuries often develop over days.

03

Does NSW's "Obvious Risk" Defence Apply To Slip And Fall Claims?

The Civil Liability Act 2002 provides a defence where the risk was an "obvious risk" — one that a reasonable person in the circumstances would have seen. However, courts regularly find that a slippery wet floor, poorly lit steps, or an unmarked hazard was not obvious enough to defeat a claim.

The obvious risk defence is more commonly argued by defendants in outdoor hazard cases than in commercial premises. Whether it applies depends on the specific circumstances, the visibility of the hazard, and what warnings (if any) were provided. We assess this at your free claim check.

04

What Compensation Can You Recover For A Slip And Fall In NSW?

Damages include past and future medical expenses, past and future lost income, the cost of care and assistance, and non-economic loss (pain and suffering) — subject to the 15% threshold under section 16 of the Civil Liability Act 2002.

Fractures to hips, wrists and ankles, spinal injuries and head trauma regularly meet the 15% threshold. Soft tissue injuries that fully resolve may not — which is another reason not to settle before your injuries have stabilised. Browse our NSW public liability cases for real outcomes.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions.

How quickly do I need to act after a slip and fall?

Immediately. CCTV footage is overwritten within 24–72 hours. Report the incident on the day, photograph the hazard, obtain the incident report number, and seek legal advice as soon as possible so we can send a preservation notice.

What if I was partly at fault for the fall?

Contributory negligence can reduce your damages proportionally but rarely eliminates your claim entirely. Courts assess each case on its facts — whether you were distracted, wearing appropriate footwear, and whether the hazard was visible all factor in.

What is the time limit for a slip and fall claim in NSW?

Generally 3 years from the date of injury under the Limitation Act 1969 (NSW). For children, the clock starts at age 18. Don't wait — evidence disappears and witnesses forget.

Do I need to prove how long the hazard was there?

In NSW, proving the hazard existed for a period long enough for the occupier to have found it (had they been inspecting properly) is an important part of establishing negligence. This is why inspection records and cleaning logs are so valuable — and why we seek them immediately.
