How Do You Lodge An icare Workers Compensation Claim In NSW?

Report the injury to your employer, obtain a certificate of capacity from your doctor, then notify your employer in writing. Your employer notifies icare. Lodge promptly — you have 6 months from the date of injury, and late claims can be rejected.

icare must make an initial liability decision within 21 days of receiving the claim. What you say in your initial statement matters: descriptions of how the injury occurred are scrutinised if a Work Injury Damages claim follows. Getting it right from the start — with legal guidance — is far easier than correcting the record later.

02

What Happens When icare Disputes Or Rejects A Claim?

If icare disputes liability, issues a section 74 notice declining payments, or stops your weekly benefits, you have the right to dispute the decision. Disputes go to the Personal Injury Commission (PIC), where a conciliator and, if needed, an arbitrator will determine the matter.

Many disputes resolve at the conciliation stage with the right medical evidence and legal preparation. The key is acting quickly — dispute deadlines are generally 30 days from the decision notice. Our team handles the PIC process entirely and funds any additional medical reports needed.

03

What Is The Section 66 Permanent Impairment Lump Sum?

Under section 66 of the Workers Compensation Act 1987, if your work injury leaves you with a permanent impairment, you are entitled to a lump sum payment. The amount is calculated by multiplying your Whole Person Impairment (WPI) percentage by the maximum compensation amount, indexed annually.

Never accept a s66 offer without first getting advice on whether a Work Injury Damages (WID) claim is available. If your WPI is 15% or more, a WID claim can be worth many times the s66 lump sum — and the interaction between s66 payments and WID damages is exactly the kind of detail that costs unrepresented workers significant money. The advice is free; the mistake isn't.

04

Can icare Make You Attend Their Doctor?

Yes. icare can require independent medical examinations (IMEs). You must attend, but you have the right to evidence from your own treating specialists, which we make sure carries proper weight in any dispute.

IME reports organised by insurers often minimise injury severity and restrict treatment approvals. We routinely obtain counter-evidence from your treating team and, where necessary, from independent specialists we brief ourselves. An insurer-appointed assessment is not the final word on your condition.

05

What If icare Stops My Weekly Payments?

Payment terminations under the NSW scheme have strict review deadlines — generally 30 days. Don't accept a cut-off at face value. Get the decision reviewed quickly, and get advice on whether a Work Injury Damages claim should be running in parallel to protect your full economic loss.

Under the Workers Compensation Act 1987, weekly payments step down over time based on your capacity for work and time since injury. Understanding where you sit in the scheme's payment structure — and whether the insurer is applying the right rules — requires specialist knowledge.
