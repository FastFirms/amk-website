What Is A Public Liability Claim In NSW?

A public liability claim is a negligence claim against a person or organisation that owes you a duty of care and failed to keep their premises or activities reasonably safe. In NSW, these claims are governed by the Civil Liability Act 2002, which sets the test for negligence and limits on damages.

Common scenarios include slips and falls in shopping centres or supermarkets, injuries in parks, sporting venues or public spaces, falls at someone's home, and injuries caused by unsafe products or equipment. The duty of care applies to occupiers, event organisers, councils, body corporates and businesses — whoever had responsibility for the area or activity that caused your injury.

02

What Must You Prove Under The Civil Liability Act 2002 (NSW)?

Under section 5B of the Civil Liability Act 2002, a defendant is negligent if: the risk of harm was foreseeable, the risk was not insignificant, and a reasonable person in their position would have taken precautions. The burden is on you to establish each element.

Even if negligence is proven, NSW's Civil Liability Act also contains proportionate liability rules, contributory negligence provisions (which can reduce your damages), and a threshold for non-economic loss (general damages for pain and suffering). NSW's threshold for non-economic loss under section 16 requires your injury to be at least 15% of the most extreme case — below this threshold, no general damages are payable. Economic loss (lost income, care costs) has no threshold and is separately assessed.

03

Who Is Liable For A Public Liability Injury In NSW?

Liability attaches to whoever owed and breached the duty of care — which can be the occupier, a contractor, an event organiser, a body corporate, a council, or more than one party at once. Identifying the correct defendant or defendants early determines the direction of the whole claim.

In James v USM Events [2023] NSWDC, a duathlon participant injured in a collision recovered $1,062,351 from the event organiser. In Cullen v State of New South Wales [2023] NSWSC, police were found liable for knocking down a bystander — $800,000. The defendant isn't always who you'd first expect. 04

What Are The Time Limits For A Public Liability Claim In NSW?

Under the Limitation Act 1969 (NSW), you generally have 3 years from the date of injury to commence proceedings. For children, the limitation period does not begin until they turn 18. For claims involving a latent injury (where the injury was not discoverable at the time), the period may run from the date of discoverability.

Evidence disappears fast — CCTV footage is overwritten within days, witnesses forget details, and hazard inspection records are harder to obtain the longer you wait. Acting quickly protects your evidence base and your claim.

05

What Compensation Can You Recover?

Damages available in NSW public liability claims include: past and future medical expenses, past and future lost income (including superannuation), the cost of past and future care and assistance, and non-economic loss (pain and suffering) — subject to the 15% threshold.

NSW's Civil Liability Act also caps non-economic loss damages at a maximum (indexed annually). The care component — even when provided informally by family members — is claimable where the care is necessary and substantial. Future economic loss can be the largest component for younger claimants with serious injuries.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions.

What law governs public liability claims in NSW?

The Civil Liability Act 2002 (NSW) governs negligence claims including public liability. It sets the test for negligence under s5B, limits on non-economic loss (15% threshold under s16), and contributory negligence rules.

How long do I have to make a claim?

Generally 3 years from the date of injury under the Limitation Act 1969 (NSW). For children, the period runs from age 18. Evidence disappears quickly — don't wait.

Can my damages be reduced for contributory negligence?

Yes. If you contributed to your own injury — for example by not watching where you were walking — your damages may be reduced proportionally. They are not wiped out entirely unless you were wholly responsible, which is rare.

What if I was injured at a friend's home?

Homeowners owe a duty of care to visitors under the Civil Liability Act. If a hazard caused your injury and the homeowner knew or should have known about it, a claim is possible. In practice, the claim is made against the homeowner's public liability insurance — not against the individual personally.
