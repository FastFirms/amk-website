What Is Medical Negligence In NSW?

Medical negligence occurs when a healthcare provider — a doctor, surgeon, nurse, dentist, hospital or allied health professional — fails to provide treatment of the standard reasonably expected of a competent professional in their field, and that failure causes you harm.

In NSW, medical negligence claims are governed by the Civil Liability Act 2002, which contains a specific professional standards provision (section 5O). Under s5O, a professional is not negligent if they acted in a manner that was widely accepted by peer professional opinion as competent professional practice — unless that practice was so unreasonable that no reasonable professional would have followed it. This is a higher bar than ordinary negligence, which is why expert evidence from qualified medical professionals in the same specialty is critical to every claim.

02

What Types Of Medical Negligence Can You Claim For In NSW?

Any failure that falls below the accepted standard of care and causes demonstrable harm. Common categories include: misdiagnosis or delayed diagnosis of serious conditions, surgical errors, anaesthetic errors, medication errors, hospital-acquired infections arising from inadequate hygiene protocols, birth injuries, and failure to obtain informed consent.

Failure to warn is a particularly important category in NSW following the High Court's decision in Rogers v Whitaker (1992). A doctor must warn of material risks of a proposed procedure — risks that a reasonable patient in your position would want to know about. Failure to disclose a material risk that eventuates can found a claim even if the procedure itself was technically performed correctly.

03

What Do You Need To Prove?

You must establish: (1) the defendant owed you a duty of care; (2) they breached the standard of care required by the Civil Liability Act; (3) that breach caused your injury or worsened your outcome (causation); and (4) you suffered loss as a result. Each element requires expert medical evidence.

Causation is often the hardest element — you must show that the negligent act or omission caused the specific harm you suffered, not merely that your outcome was poor. We brief leading specialists to provide opinions on both breach and causation before any claim is commenced. Cases without solid expert support rarely succeed.

04

What Are The Time Limits For A Medical Negligence Claim In NSW?

Under the Limitation Act 1969 (NSW), the limitation period for personal injury claims is generally 3 years from the date the cause of action was discoverable — that is, when you knew or ought to have known that you had suffered harm, that a particular person caused it, and that the harm was sufficiently serious to warrant legal action.

For claims involving latent conditions (where harm becomes apparent years after the negligent act), the discoverability rule often extends the limitation period significantly. An overriding 12-year long-stop period applies. For children, the period runs from age 18. If you are uncertain whether your limitation period has expired, get advice — the answer is not always obvious.

05

What Compensation Is Available?

Damages in NSW medical negligence claims include: past and future medical expenses (including the cost of corrective treatment), past and future lost income, the cost of ongoing care and assistance, and non-economic loss (pain, suffering, loss of amenity) — subject to the 15% threshold under the Civil Liability Act.

Cases involving permanent disability, loss of a limb or organ, significant cognitive impairment, or the death of a parent can attract substantial awards. FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions.

What law governs medical negligence claims in NSW?

The Civil Liability Act 2002 (NSW), including the professional standards defence under section 5O. Medical negligence claims also require analysis under common law principles from Rogers v Whitaker (1992) HCA regarding informed consent.

How long do I have to bring a medical negligence claim?

Generally 3 years from the date the harm was discoverable under the Limitation Act 1969 (NSW). A 12-year long-stop period also applies. If you're unsure whether your limitation period has passed, get advice — the discoverability rule often extends time further than patients expect.

Do I need my own medical expert?

Yes. Every successful medical negligence claim requires expert opinion from a qualified professional in the same specialty as the defendant, confirming both breach of the standard of care and causation. We brief appropriate specialists as part of your claim — we fund these reports upfront on a No Win, No Fee basis.

What if the bad outcome might have happened anyway?

This is the causation question. You must show the negligence materially contributed to your harm — that without the breach, your outcome would have been better. We assess this carefully with expert help before advising you to proceed.
