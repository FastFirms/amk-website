What Personal Injury Claims Are Available In NSW?

NSW has several distinct compensation schemes depending on how you were injured. Motor vehicle accidents run under the Motor Accidents Injuries Act 2017. Workplace injuries run under the Workers Compensation Act 1987 with icare as the main insurer. Public place injuries and medical negligence run under the Civil Liability Act 2002. TPD and superannuation claims are governed by federal law.

Each scheme has different rules, time limits, thresholds and processes. Getting into the right scheme — and understanding how they interact if you have multiple injuries or multiple potential defendants — is where an experienced personal injury lawyer adds the most value. Our free claim check identifies which scheme applies to you and what your claim could be worth.

02

What Compensation Can You Claim In NSW?

Across all NSW personal injury schemes, damages can include: past and future medical and rehabilitation expenses, past and future lost income (including superannuation), the cost of care and assistance, and non-economic loss (pain, suffering and loss of amenity of life) — subject to the relevant scheme's threshold.

Non-economic loss in NSW public liability and medical negligence cases requires meeting the 15% threshold under the Civil Liability Act 2002. In CTP claims, non-economic loss requires 10% Whole Person Impairment. Workers compensation WID claims require 15% WPI. Understanding which threshold applies — and whether your injuries meet it — is assessed at your free consultation.

03

What Are The Time Limits For Personal Injury Claims In NSW?

Time limits vary critically by scheme. CTP: Accident Notification Form 28 days, Notice of Claim 3 months. Workers comp: lodge statutory claim within 6 months, WID within 3 years. Public liability and medical negligence: generally 3 years from discoverability under the Limitation Act 1969 (NSW). Missing these deadlines can permanently bar your claim.

Don't assume you have time to think about it. Evidence disappears, witnesses forget, and missed deadlines require court applications that are not guaranteed to succeed. Our first consultation is free — use it before a deadline passes.

04

Can You Claim For Psychological Injuries In NSW?

Yes. Psychological injuries — PTSD, depression, anxiety disorders — are compensable in NSW in every scheme. In CTP and public liability claims they are treated like physical injuries. In workers compensation, psychological injuries face the additional 'reasonable management action' defence. All require robust psychiatric evidence.

Psychological injuries are often undervalued by insurers because they are not visible. We routinely commission independent psychiatric assessments to ensure your psychological harm is properly documented and fully compensated.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions.

Do I have a personal injury claim in NSW?

If someone else's negligence caused your injury — a driver, employer, business, property owner or health provider — you likely have a claim. The fastest way to know is our free claim check: two minutes online, or a free consultation by phone, video or in person across NSW.

Can I claim for psychological injuries?

Yes. Psychological injuries are compensable in NSW across all personal injury schemes. Workers compensation claims face the 'reasonable management action' defence. All require robust psychiatric evidence, which we arrange and fund.

What are the time limits in NSW?

CTP: ANF 28 days, Notice of Claim 3 months. Workers comp: 6 months to lodge. Public liability and medical negligence: 3 years from discoverability. Missing deadlines can bar your claim permanently — get advice early.

Will making a claim affect the person I'm claiming against?

In almost all NSW personal injury cases, compensation is paid by an insurer — CTP, icare, or public liability insurer — not by the individual personally.

How long will my claim take?

Most NSW personal injury claims resolve within 12 to 24 months once injuries stabilise. We'll give you a realistic timeframe at your free appraisal.
